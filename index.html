When silence was not an option: lessons from communicating through a crisis
Geetika Yadav · 5 min read

In early 2020, my team had 72 hours to build a communications plan for 2,000+ employees. No playbook. No precedent.

Over the next 12 months, we delivered 50+ targeted communications across 5 channels and sustained an ~80% employee awareness score. Three things made the difference:

Clarity beats comprehensiveness. Each message had one primary action or insight. Writing for a single question — "What does this mean for me, right now?" — drove clarity and cut noise.

Cadence is its own form of reassurance. Extending lead time from 2 to 10 days created space for alignment, better editing, and consistency — instead of publishing under pressure.

The communicator's job is to reduce cognitive load, not add to it. Short paragraphs. Consistent formats. Named contacts. Specific timelines. These are not stylistic choices. They are acts of organisational care.

One habit that improved every presentation I worked on
Before writing a single slide, write one sentence: "After this presentation, the audience will ___."

Not "understand our Q3 results." A specific, concrete action.

Most presentations are built around what the presenter knows — not what the audience needs to leave with.

Try it on your next deck. You'll cut at least three slides.

The most underrated skill: knowing when not to send
More volume does not equal more trust. We measured it.

When we reduced frequency and increased specificity, response rates went up. Fatigue dropped. Managers stopped forwarding with 'FYI' and started engaging.

If employees aren't reading your comms, the answer is rarely more communications.
