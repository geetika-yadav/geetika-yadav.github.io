About

Communications as a craft, not a function

I am a communications and content professional with over a decade of experience building communication systems that work: for leaders who need to be heard, for employees who need clarity, and for organisations navigating change.

My work spans executive communications, internal campaigns, change management, content strategy, and digital channels. I have led communications for enterprise-wide initiatives at Genpact, managed intranet platforms for 2,000+ employees, and delivered programmes under pressure, including a 12-month COVID transition that became one of the most sustained internal communications efforts I have worked on.

I hold an MBA in International Marketing from IU International University of Applied Sciences, Germany, which gave formal rigour to instincts I had been developing for years about audience, message, and impact.

What I believe: the best communication is not the most frequent or the most elaborate. It is the communication that arrives at the right moment, in the right format, with exactly what people need to hear.

Sample 02
Internal newsletter: design and editorial structure
A designed newsletter template showing how I approach layout, content hierarchy, editorial tone, and audience segmentation for a recurring internal publication.
Newsletter design
1
The brief
Design a recurring internal newsletter for a programme team of 2,000+ employees. It needs to carry multiple content types, feel consistent across issues, and not require a designer to produce each time.
2
The approach
Defined four fixed zones: a hero story, a two-column news section, a data snapshot row, and a footer. Fixed zones mean the template can be populated by any team member without layout decisions. Editorial tone set as direct and human, not corporate.
3
Format decision
Brand-consistent header with issue numbering so employees build a habit of looking for it. Colour-coded content tags so readers can navigate to what is relevant to them. Stats row carries the key pulse data in every issue, making numbers part of the rhythm.

Executive communication: programme launch announcement
A designed email template for a leadership announcement, showing how I structure executive voice, visual hierarchy, and clear messaging across a complex communication.
Executive comms
1
The brief
The COO needs to announce a major programme to all employees. The message must be reassuring without being vague, credible without being dense, and easy to act on without being patronising.
2
The approach
Three content blocks answer the three questions employees ask first: what is this, why now, and what this is not. The third block (what this is not) is the most important, as it addresses the fear before the employee has to voice it.
3
Format decision
Branded email template with a clear sender identity, subject line designed to be opened, three equal-weight content blocks for scanning, and a single next-step action. No attachments on first send, all detail lives on the intranet page.

Presentation design
1
The brief
Build one slide for a leadership deck that communicates programme progress at the 6-month mark. The audience is senior leadership. The slide needs to convey confidence without oversimplifying, and carry a clear narrative, not just data.
2
The approach
The left panel carries the narrative: what is the situation, what has been achieved, and what it means. The right panel carries the evidence: four KPIs that back the narrative. The two panels are designed to work independently so a presenter can reference either without the other.

Sample 05
SharePoint homepage: zone architecture and wireframe
A structured homepage design for a programme intranet, showing zone planning, navigation logic, content ownership, and publishing governance.
SharePoint design
1
The brief
Design a SharePoint homepage for a major programme that serves 2,000+ employees across multiple functions. Must work without a developer after initial build, support a weekly publishing cadence, and be governable by a comms team of two.
2
The approach
Mapped the six distinct audience needs: knowing what is happening now, understanding the programme structure, finding documents, tracking the timeline, knowing who to contact, and attending events. Each need maps to one homepage zone, so the architecture is audience-driven rather than content-driven.
3
Governance model
Every zone has a named owner. The Comms Lead owns the hero and news zones. Workstream Leads own their tile subsites. Nothing publishes without a named owner assigned. Stale content (over 30 days without review) is flagged automatically.

One habit that improved every presentation I worked on
Before writing a single slide, write one sentence: "After this presentation, the audience will ___."

Not "understand our Q3 results." A specific, concrete action or decision.

Most presentations are built around what the presenter knows, not what the audience needs to leave with.

Try it on your next deck. You will cut at least three slides.
