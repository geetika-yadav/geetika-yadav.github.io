Communicating through a crisis: a written article

A long-form piece drawing on the COVID-19 programme experience, demonstrating how I structure written thought leadership for a professional audience.

1
The brief
Write a professional article grounded in real experience rather than theory. Audience: peers, hiring managers, and potential collaborators who understand communications.
2
The approach
Three concrete lessons, each backed by a specific operational decision from the programme. Reader gets insight, not assertion. Opening hook drops the reader into the moment before any context is given.
3
Format decision
Long-form with numbered insights for scannability. Closes with a philosophy rather than a summary. Designed to be read in full, not skimmed.

1. Clarity beats comprehensiveness

Our first instinct was to include everything in every message. It did not work. The shift happened when we stopped trying to be comprehensive and started writing for one question at a time: "What does this mean for me, right now?" From that point on, each communication had a single primary action or insight. Everything else lived on the intranet or waited for the next message in the series.

10 days
Average campaign lead time, extended from 2. That single operational shift created space for proper stakeholder alignment before every send.
2. Cadence is its own form of reassurance

Employees were not anxious about the news. They were anxious about the silence between the news. A predictable rhythm of weekly updates and fortnightly leadership messages reduced informal speculation and helped people feel the organisation was still functioning with intention. Extending lead time from 2 to 10 days made a meaningful difference in message quality and stakeholder confidence.

3. The communicator's job is to reduce cognitive load, not add to it

During a crisis, every person on your audience list is already processing uncertainty. Your job is not to demonstrate how much you know. It is to reduce the work they have to do to understand what is happening and what they need to do next. Short paragraphs. Consistent formats. Named contacts. Specific timelines. These are not stylistic choices. They are acts of organisational care.

Sample 02Newsletter design

Internal newsletter as it lands in an Outlook inbox

A designed recurring newsletter rendered exactly as it would appear when an employee opens it in Microsoft Outlook, showing editorial structure, branded layout, and content hierarchy.

1
The brief
Design a recurring internal newsletter for 2,000+ employees. Needs to carry multiple content types, feel consistent across issues, and not require a designer to produce each time.
2
The approach
Four fixed zones: hero story, two-column news, data snapshot, and footer. Fixed zones mean the template can be populated by any team member without layout decisions each time. Editorial tone is direct and human.
3
Format decision
Issue numbering so employees build a habit of looking for it. Colour-coded content tags for navigation. Stats row carries key pulse data in every issue, making numbers part of the rhythm rather than a special feature.

Sample 03Executive communication

Executive programme launch announcement as it appears in Outlook

A leadership communication from the COO announcing a major transformation programme, rendered as it would appear in an employee's Outlook inbox with full branded email template.

1
The brief
The COO needs to announce a major programme to all employees. The message must be reassuring without being vague, credible without being dense, and easy to act on without being patronising.
2
The approach
Three content blocks answer the questions employees ask first: what is this, why now, and what this is not. The third block addresses the fear before the employee has to voice it.
3
Format decision
Branded template with a clear sender identity, subject line designed to be opened, and a single next-step action. No attachments on first send. All detail lives on the intranet page.

Executive presentation slide as it appears in PowerPoint

A mid-programme progress slide from a leadership deck, shown inside the PowerPoint application interface to demonstrate how it would actually be presented to senior leadership.

1
The brief
One slide for a leadership deck communicating programme progress at the 6-month mark. Senior audience. Must convey confidence without oversimplifying, and carry a clear narrative, not just data.
2
The approach
Left panel carries the narrative: situation, achievement, and what it means. Right panel carries the evidence: four KPIs that back the narrative. Both panels work independently so the presenter can reference either without the other.

Sample 05SharePoint design

SharePoint homepage as it appears in a browser

A programme intranet homepage rendered inside a realistic browser chrome with the full SharePoint interface, showing zone architecture, navigation, and content governance in context.

1
The brief
Design a SharePoint homepage for a major programme serving 2,000+ employees. Must work without a developer after initial build, support a weekly publishing cadence, and be governable by a comms team of two.
2
The approach
Mapped six distinct audience needs to six homepage zones. Architecture is audience-driven rather than content-driven. Each zone has a named owner. Nothing publishes without ownership accountability.
3
Format decision
Hero zone carries the current phase and a single CTA. News zone surfaces only the three most recent items, auto-sorted. Timeline tracker gives employees a live progress view without requiring them to read a document.

One habit that improves every executive presentation
Before writing a single slide, write one sentence: "After this presentation, the audience will ___."

Not "understand our Q3 results." A specific, concrete outcome or decision.

Most decks are built around what the presenter knows. The best ones are built around what the audience needs to leave with.

It sounds simple. But it changes everything about how you structure content, what you cut, and where you start.

We use this as the first exercise in every executive comms workshop we run.

The most overlooked lever in change communications: the calendar
50+ communications. 5 channels. An awareness score of approximately 80% sustained across a 12-month enterprise programme.

The biggest difference was not the content. It was the lead time.

Moving from 2-day to 10-day campaign lead times created space for stakeholder alignment, better editing, and consistent channel decisions — rather than publishing under pressure.

When communications feel reactive, it is usually a planning problem, not a content problem.

Fix the calendar first.
